===== DISEASE: Atelectasis =====

OVERVIEW:
Atelectasis refers to partial or complete collapse of alveolar air
spaces, resulting in reduced lung volume and impaired gas exchange.
It may involve a segment, lobe, or entire lung.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Chest radiographs commonly demonstrate increased opacity in the
affected region accompanied by evidence of volume loss. Typical
features include displacement of interlobar fissures, elevation of
the ipsilateral hemidiaphragm, narrowing of intercostal spaces, and
mediastinal shift toward the affected side. Crowding of pulmonary
vessels and bronchi may also be present.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Clinical presentation ranges from asymptomatic findings to dyspnea,
tachypnea, and hypoxemia, particularly in extensive or acute cases.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Atelectasis is frequently encountered in postoperative patients,
those with airway obstruction, prolonged immobilization, or mucus
plugging. Persistent atelectasis may predispose to infection.

LIMITATIONS:
Radiographic differentiation from consolidation may be challenging,
especially without longitudinal imaging or clinical correlation.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Cardiomegaly =====

OVERVIEW:
Cardiomegaly describes enlargement of the cardiac silhouette observed
on chest radiographs, reflecting increased cardiac size or pericardial
abnormalities.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
An increased cardiothoracic ratio exceeding approximately 50 percent
on posteroanterior chest radiographs is suggestive. Associated findings
may include pulmonary venous congestion, interstitial edema, or pleural
effusions in advanced cases.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Symptoms may include exertional dyspnea, fatigue, peripheral edema,
orthopnea, or may be absent depending on underlying cardiac function.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Cardiomegaly may indicate underlying cardiac pathology such as heart
failure, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, or chronic hypertension.

LIMITATIONS:
Apparent cardiomegaly may result from anteroposterior projection,
suboptimal inspiration, or pericardial effusion, requiring further
evaluation.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pleural Effusion =====

OVERVIEW:
Pleural effusion refers to abnormal accumulation of fluid within the
pleural space between the visceral and parietal pleura.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Radiographic features include blunting of the costophrenic angles,
homogeneous basilar opacities, and a meniscus sign. Large effusions may
cause mediastinal shift and compress adjacent lung tissue.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Patients may experience dyspnea, chest discomfort, or cough, though
small effusions are frequently asymptomatic.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Pleural effusions may arise from cardiac failure, infection, malignancy,
trauma, or systemic inflammatory processes.

LIMITATIONS:
Small or loculated effusions may be difficult to detect on standard
upright chest radiographs.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Infiltration =====

OVERVIEW:
Pulmonary infiltration denotes the presence of abnormal material such
as fluid, inflammatory cells, or blood within the lung interstitium
or alveolar spaces.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Chest radiographs reveal ill-defined, patchy, or diffuse opacities
without clear lobar or segmental boundaries. Distribution may be
unilateral or bilateral.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Clinical manifestations vary widely and may include cough, fever,
dyspnea, or systemic symptoms depending on etiology.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Infiltrates represent a nonspecific radiographic pattern and require
integration with clinical and laboratory data for interpretation.

LIMITATIONS:
Radiographic appearance alone cannot determine underlying cause or
severity.

SOURCE:
Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Mass =====

OVERVIEW:
A pulmonary mass is defined as a focal lung lesion typically measuring
greater than 3 centimeters in diameter.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Masses appear as rounded or irregular opacities with variable margins,
which may be smooth, lobulated, or spiculated. Cavitation or associated
lymphadenopathy may occasionally be observed.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Patients may be asymptomatic or present with cough, hemoptysis, chest
pain, or unexplained weight loss.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Pulmonary masses raise concern for malignancy and warrant further
diagnostic evaluation.

LIMITATIONS:
Chest radiography provides limited lesion characterization; cross-
sectional imaging is often required.

SOURCE:
Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Nodule =====

OVERVIEW:
A pulmonary nodule is a small, rounded opacity within the lung
parenchyma measuring less than 3 centimeters in diameter.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Discrete, well-circumscribed opacities that may be solitary or multiple.
Calcification patterns, when visible, may suggest benignity.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Pulmonary nodules are commonly asymptomatic and incidentally detected.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Nodules may represent benign granulomas, infections, or early-stage
malignancy and often require surveillance.

LIMITATIONS:
Small nodules may be below the detection threshold of standard chest
radiographs.

SOURCE:
Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pneumonia =====

OVERVIEW:
Pneumonia is an inflammatory condition of the lung parenchyma most
commonly caused by infectious agents.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Chest radiographs may demonstrate focal, multifocal, or diffuse areas
of consolidation, air bronchograms, or increased lung opacity.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Typical symptoms include fever, productive cough, dyspnea, and pleuritic
chest pain.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Radiographic findings must be interpreted in conjunction with clinical
presentation and laboratory investigations.

LIMITATIONS:
Early or mild pneumonia may not be radiographically apparent.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pneumothorax =====

OVERVIEW:
Pneumothorax is defined by the presence of air within the pleural space,
resulting in partial or complete lung collapse.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
A visible visceral pleural line with absence of lung markings beyond it
is characteristic. Large pneumothoraces may cause mediastinal shift.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Symptoms range from mild chest discomfort to acute respiratory distress,
depending on size and rate of onset.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Prompt recognition is important due to potential for rapid clinical
deterioration.

LIMITATIONS:
Small pneumothoraces may be occult on supine chest radiographs.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Consolidation =====

OVERVIEW:
Pulmonary consolidation occurs when alveolar air is replaced by fluid,
pus, blood, or cellular material.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Homogeneous increased opacity obscuring bronchovascular markings, often
confined to a segment or lobe.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Symptoms depend on underlying cause and may include fever, cough, or
dyspnea.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Most commonly associated with infectious processes but may reflect other
pathologies.

LIMITATIONS:
Radiographic appearance alone cannot establish etiology.

SOURCE:
Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Edema =====

OVERVIEW:
Pulmonary edema involves abnormal accumulation of fluid within the lung
interstitium and alveoli.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Bilateral perihilar opacities, interstitial markings, Kerley B lines,
and a bat-wing distribution may be observed.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Patients often present with acute dyspnea, orthopnea, and hypoxemia.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Frequently associated with cardiac dysfunction or volume overload.

LIMITATIONS:
Radiographic findings may lag behind clinical presentation.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Emphysema =====

OVERVIEW:
Emphysema is a chronic lung condition characterized by destruction of
alveolar walls and enlargement of distal air spaces.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Hyperinflated lungs, flattened diaphragms, increased retrosternal air
space, and reduced vascular markings are typical.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Progressive dyspnea, reduced exercise tolerance, and chronic cough are
common.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
A major component of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.

LIMITATIONS:
Early disease may not be radiographically visible.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pulmonary Fibrosis =====

OVERVIEW:
Pulmonary fibrosis involves progressive scarring of lung parenchyma
leading to reduced lung compliance.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Reticular opacities, architectural distortion, traction bronchiectasis,
and reduced lung volumes may be seen.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Chronic cough and progressive exertional dyspnea are typical.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
Associated with chronic interstitial lung diseases and environmental
exposures.

LIMITATIONS:
Chest X-ray has limited sensitivity compared to high-resolution CT.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Pleural Thickening =====

OVERVIEW:
Pleural thickening refers to fibrotic involvement of the pleural
surfaces.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Localized or diffuse pleural opacities along the chest wall or
diaphragm, often with irregular margins.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Often asymptomatic; may cause restrictive respiratory symptoms if
extensive.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
May result from prior infection, trauma, or asbestos exposure.

LIMITATIONS:
Subtle thickening may be difficult to detect radiographically.

SOURCE:
Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14


===== DISEASE: Diaphragmatic Hernia =====

OVERVIEW:
Diaphragmatic hernia involves herniation of abdominal contents into
the thoracic cavity through a defect in the diaphragm.

RADIOLOGICAL FINDINGS:
Abnormal gas patterns or soft tissue densities above the diaphragm,
sometimes with displacement of thoracic structures.

SYMPTOM DESCRIPTIONS:
Symptoms range from incidental findings to respiratory compromise,
depending on size and contents.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
May be congenital or acquired and can impact pulmonary mechanics.

LIMITATIONS:
Small hernias may be difficult to visualize on chest radiographs.

SOURCE:
NIH MedlinePlus; Radiopaedia; ChestX-ray14
